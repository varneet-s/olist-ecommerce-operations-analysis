# Insights

All four business questions were confirmed. Each finding points back to the same
structural problem — Olist's seller base, logistics reach, and operational focus
were concentrated in São Paulo and the southeast during 2016–2018. Everything else
flows from that.

---

## H1 — Late Deliveries Cause Satisfaction Collapse, Not Just Lower Scores

On-time deliveries averaged **4.03** review score. Late deliveries averaged **2.27** —
a 1.76-point gap on a 5-point scale. Orders 7+ days late dropped further to **1.70**.

This is not a marginal difference. Below 3.0, a customer is not just dissatisfied —
they are unlikely to return and may actively leave negative feedback on the marketplace
platform. Delivery timing turned out to be the single biggest driver of customer
satisfaction in this dataset, above price or product quality.

**What this means:** Sellers in remote regions are receiving low review scores for a
logistics problem they did not create. That is both unfair to sellers and damaging
to Olist's platform reputation.

---

## H2 — Revenue Concentration in the Southeast Is a Business Risk

São Paulo alone generated **42% of total platform revenue**. The top 3 states —
SP, RJ, MG — accounted for approximately **70% of GMV**.

This level of concentration means platform performance is tied to one regional economy.
A competitive entrant targeting São Paulo specifically, or any regional economic
disruption, would have an outsized impact on Olist's total revenue. The rest of
Brazil is underserved — not because demand doesn't exist, but because the platform
hasn't reached it yet.

---

## H3 — 20% of Sellers Generate 82.5% of GMV

Of 2,960 sellers, the top **592** generated **82.54% of total GMV**. That is a
stronger concentration than the classic 80/20 split.

The platform's commercial performance depends heavily on retaining a small seller
cohort. If a meaningful portion of those top sellers reduced activity or moved to
a competitor, GMV would drop sharply. The remaining 80% of sellers are not failing
— they are constrained by the same freight and delivery problems affecting their
customers.

---

## H4 — Northern Customers Pay Freight Costs That Make Purchases Economically Difficult

Freight in northern states is not marginally higher — it is in a different category:

- Roraima: ~60% of product price
- Rondônia: ~58% of product price
- Maranhão: ~54% of product price
- São Paulo: ~25% of product price

A customer in Roraima buying a R$100 product pays R$160 total. At that cost, demand
is suppressed — not because the product is unwanted, but because the economics make
the purchase difficult to justify.

**Critical observation:** Despite these costs, customers in northern states were still
placing orders. That indicates real latent demand being throttled by logistics
economics, not lack of interest in the platform.

---

## Insight 5 — These Are Not Four Separate Problems

Each finding above has the same root cause:

- Fewer sellers in the north → longer distances between seller and customer
- Longer distances → higher freight costs and slower transit
- Slower transit → more late deliveries
- Late deliveries → lower review scores
- High freight + low scores → suppressed demand in northern states
- Suppressed demand → revenue stays concentrated in SP

Solving freight, delivery, and revenue concentration as three separate initiatives
will not work. The underlying problem is seller distribution — and that is where
any meaningful intervention has to start.
